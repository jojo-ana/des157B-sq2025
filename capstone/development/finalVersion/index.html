The whispers of the American Dream began in the 1930s, where individuals seeked personal fulfillment and self-expression; the rhetoric of the American Identity

Amid economic instability from the 1929 Stock Market Crash and a shifting political climate with President Roosevelt’s New Deal looking for recovery and reform, people held tightly to hope, refusing to let go of the belief in a better future.

There was a shift from pursuing a path of wealth to one of stability to trying to find a way for resilience and recovery.

James Truslow Adams coined the phrase “The American Dream” in his 1931 book The Epic of America as an idealization of life in America. But what does this mean to someone of the past?

At the heart, it's the collective hope that people can rise above their circumstances. In this vision, the dream is not solely about personal success, but about contributing to the welfare and progress of society as a whole.

Scroll

The social side of the American Dream showed that real progress comes when people work together. It meant changing how we see gender roles, standing up for workers’ rights, and pushing back against racial injustice. These efforts proved that no one rises alone — we grow by lifting each other up.

It is in this spirit of togetherness that the dream truly took root, reminding us that true greatness is realized not alone, but through the bonds we forge and the communities we built.

THIS IS AMERICA

202X

Scroll

Transformed from American stability to one of American exceptionalism–a narrative of shared experiences but ambiguous to make it seem it can be applied to all. However, the mental model has shifted from collective growth to one of individual growth, how one can achieve their American Dream. America is a land of possibility and opportunity, allowing for social mobility and determining your own fate through hard work.

But the dream comes at a cost, literally.

As society places more focus on individual success, people are more likely to feel disconnected and anxious, with shared values and a sense of belonging gradually fading away.

James Truslow Adams warned of a future where workers blindly follow a capitalist system, convinced it will lead to success, yet unaware of how it often exploits them. Today, that warning rings true.

Many continue to chase the promise of upward mobility, believing hard work alone guarantees prosperity. But the system they participate in too often rewards wealth over labor, leaving workers undervalued and undercompensated. The ideal of the self-made millionaire has become the modern version of the American Dream, something marketed as attainable, yet realistically out of reach for most.

Capitalism has created great wealth, but it’s not shared equally. Those born into privilege have access to better education, networks, and safety nets, making success easier. Meanwhile, low-income people face constant barriers, stuck in a cycle where the rich keep rising and the working class stays in place, no matter how hard they work.

Scroll

As inequality grows and it becomes harder to move up in society, real risks start to appear. Many young people, weighed down by debt, high rent, and low pay, feel shut out of stable futures. This frustration leads to distrust, divides communities, and fuels movements that offer quick fixes instead of real solutions. If nothing changes, it won’t just hurt the economy — it could weaken our democracy too.

The redistribution of wealth isn't about punishment but about balance. When opportunities are shared more broadly, communities are stabilized and economic growth becomes more sustainable. Reforming the financial system means limiting hoarding and giving everyone a fair shot at success.

True strength lies in community. When people feel connected, they’re more resilient, healthier, and hopeful. It's a version of the American Dream that isn't abstract or unreachable but tangible, something we would live daily.

It's not confined to slogans or distant ideals, it takes shape in local schools that are well-funded, in accessible healthcare, in affordable housing, and in streets where neighbors know and look out for each other.
